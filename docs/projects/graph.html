The Problem: AI is Disrupting Education (And We're Handling It Wrong)

The Education Crisis: Students can complete entire assignments using AI, but most educators just ban everything instead of building responsible integration.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: students can now complete entire assignments using AI tools, and most educators' response has been to panic and ban everything. The result? A "plague upon education" mentality that treats AI as the enemy rather than asking the harder question: how do we actually integrate these tools responsibly?

[Screenshot: Traditional educational AI - generic chatbot interface]

Before: Students getting easy answers without understanding concepts

This isn't just a policy problem – it's a technical one. When students inevitably use AI anyway (and they will), we need systems that can guide them toward genuine learning rather than academic shortcuts.

That's particularly true in NLP courses, where the irony is thick: we're teaching students about the very technology they're tempted to misuse. But this also makes NLP classes perfect testing grounds for figuring out what responsible AI integration actually looks like.

Why Traditional Approaches Miss the Point

Most educational AI systems are just glorified search engines with chatbot interfaces. They'll retrieve relevant documents and generate plausible-sounding answers, but they can't understand the deeper relationships between concepts or adapt their responses to different types of learning needs.

The problem runs deeper than just retrieval quality. Traditional RAG systems work fine when you need to find specific facts, but education requires something different: the ability to trace conceptual connections, explain why ideas relate to each other, and provide explanations at the right level of detail for the student's current understanding.

Building Something That Actually Teaches

I built this system for CS 4740/5740 (Introduction to NLP) at Cornell, working with Claire Cardie. The goal was to create an AI teaching assistant that could integrate with real classroom discussions while maintaining academic integrity and promoting genuine learning.

The Architecture: Graph RAG Before Microsoft Made It Famous

This was spring 2024, after Harvard's CS50 paper but before every startup had a "GraphRAG" product. The key insight was that knowledge graphs could capture the structured relationships between NLP concepts in ways that pure vector search couldn't.

[System Architecture: Knowledge Graph + Vector Search + Graph Traversal]

GPT-4 extraction → Neo4j storage → Hybrid retrieval → Adaptive responses

Technical Implementation

Knowledge Graph Construction: I used GPT-3.5/GPT-4 to automatically extract entities and relationships from course lecture documents, generating Cypher queries to populate a Neo4j database. This wasn't just about storing information – it was about capturing how concepts relate to each other in the context of NLP education.

Hybrid Retrieval Strategy:

  • Vector search for semantic similarity
  • Keyword search for exact matches
  • Graph traversal for conceptual relationships

Adaptive Response Generation: Depending on the question type, the system adjusts its graph traversal depth and breadth. Theory questions need deeper conceptual connections; clarification questions benefit from exploring adjacent concepts.

What the Numbers Actually Mean

The system excelled at theory-based and conceptual integration questions, which makes sense – these are exactly the scenarios where graph-based knowledge representation shines. It could trace connections between transformer architectures and earlier sequence models, or explain how attention mechanisms relate to alignment problems.

The lower performance on circumstantial questions revealed a key limitation: the system struggled with the contextual richness of actual classroom discourse. When students asked "you mentioned a problem with attention in Thursday's lecture," the system could explain attention mechanisms perfectly but missed the specific conversational context.

Beyond the Demo: What Educational AI Actually Requires

Building this taught me that educational AI faces challenges that don't exist in other domains. It's not enough to provide accurate information – you need to provide information that promotes learning.

"Finally, an AI that doesn't just give me the answer but helps me understand how the concepts connect. Game-changer for studying NLP."

— CS 4740 Student, Spring 2024

Key Insights:

  • Evaluation is everything: Traditional metrics like ROUGE completely miss what matters in education
  • Context beats accuracy: 85% accuracy with explanation beats 95% black-box responses
  • Question types matter: Different learning objectives require different AI approaches
  • Pedagogical value ≠ Information accuracy: You need domain experts to validate educational impact

I developed automated classifiers for factuality, completeness, coherence, and relevance, but the real insights came from expert assessment. Teaching assistants and domain experts evaluated responses on pedagogical value, not just accuracy.

The Bigger Picture: Responsible AI in Education

This project was really about a larger question: how do we deploy AI in education responsibly? The technical challenges were interesting, but the ethical and pedagogical challenges were harder.

[Concept: EDGuard Smart Filter System]

AI moderation layer: Question analysis → Response filtering → Learning guidance

That's why I proposed EDGuard – a smart filter system that would moderate interactions between students and the AI. Instead of just providing answers, EDGuard would analyze both questions and responses to ensure appropriate educational engagement.

The goal wasn't to replace human instruction but to augment it. Give students access to sophisticated help when TAs aren't available, but in ways that still require them to think, synthesize, and understand.

Looking Forward: From Education to Broader AI Alignment

This work directly connects to what I'm doing now in my research at Cornell. The challenges of building AI systems that actually help rather than just providing easy answers are the same challenges we face in AI alignment more broadly.

How do you build systems that can reason about the effects of their responses, not just their accuracy? How do you create AI that promotes human capability rather than replacing it? These questions matter whether you're building educational AI or any system that needs to work beneficially with humans.

The evaluation frameworks I developed here – particularly the idea that you need to measure systems across different types of tasks and contexts – directly inform my current work on evaluating reasoning capabilities in language models.

Most importantly, this project convinced me that the most interesting AI research happens at the intersection of technical capability and human values. Building AI that actually helps education requires understanding both the technology and what education is trying to accomplish.
