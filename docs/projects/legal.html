The Problem: Legal Research is Broken (And Expensive)

The Cost Crisis: Lawyers spend 30-40% of billable time just finding relevant cases, while 86% of low-income Americans can't access adequate legal help.

Here's a stat that'll make you wince: lawyers spend 30-40% of their billable time just finding relevant cases. That's not analyzing them, not crafting arguments ‚Äì just hunting through databases with glorified keyword searches that treat "reasonable doubt" the same as "reasonable person."

Most legal tech companies have been throwing fancy interfaces at this problem for decades, but they're still doing the same thing underneath ‚Äì statistical relevance matching. It's like using a really sophisticated hammer when what you need is a surgeon's scalpel.

The real kicker? This isn't just inefficient ‚Äì it's creating an access problem. When 86% of low-income Americans can't get adequate legal help, part of the issue is that even basic legal research takes forever and costs a fortune.

Why This Gets Complicated Fast

Legal reasoning isn't like searching for restaurants or shopping for shoes. When a judge cites Warner-Jenkinson Co. v. Hilton Davis Chemical Co., they're not just saying "here's a related case." They're building an argument: this case established principle X, which applies to our facts because of Y, which leads to conclusion Z.

[Diagram: Legal reasoning chain visualization]

How legal arguments actually connect: Case A ‚Üí Principle X ‚Üí Facts Y ‚Üí Conclusion Z

But current systems can't follow that logic. They'll find the case, sure, but they can't tell you why it matters or how it connects to seventeen other precedents in the reasoning chain.

Legal language is also deliberately precise in ways that trip up standard NLP models. "Shall" vs. "may" isn't just style ‚Äì it's the difference between mandatory and permissive legal requirements. Context isn't just helpful, it's everything.

Building Something That Actually Reasons

I built LegalReasoner to tackle this systematically. Instead of just improving search, I focused on understanding the relationships between legal concepts and building tools that could follow legal reasoning across multiple documents.

The goal was to create a system that could process legal documents, understand their relationships and precedents, extract key entities, build a citation network, and answer complex legal queries with proper citations and reasoning chains.

The Technical Approach: Hybrid AI

Here's where it gets interesting. Most legal AI is either purely rule-based (transparent but rigid) or purely statistical (flexible but opaque). I went hybrid.

[System Architecture: Rule-based + AI + Network Analysis]

Deterministic citation extraction + GPT-4 reasoning + NetworkX analysis

The system combines:

  • Deterministic components for citation extraction and network construction (using the CourtListener API for ground truth)
  • Probabilistic AI (GPT-4) for reasoning analysis and natural language explanations
  • Network analysis tools (NetworkX) to model the complex relationships between cases, concepts, and legal principles

This means you get the reliability of traditional legal databases with the reasoning capabilities of modern LLMs, but with built-in transparency about where every conclusion comes from.

Beyond the Demo: What I Learned

Building this taught me that the gap between "cool AI demo" and "actually useful legal tool" is massive. The technical challenges were interesting, but the evaluation challenges were harder.

Key Lessons:

  • Evaluation is everything: How do you measure whether an AI system correctly identifies legal reasoning?
  • Domain expertise matters: You need legal professionals to validate reasoning accuracy
  • Transparency beats accuracy: Lawyers prefer explainable 85% accuracy over black-box 95%
  • Integration is key: The best AI tool is useless if it doesn't fit existing workflows

The system currently handles 1st-degree citation networks well, but legal reasoning often involves complex multi-step inferences across different areas of law. The broader goal became clear: building AI systems that can augment human legal reasoning rather than replace it.

Looking Forward: The Bigger Picture

LegalReasoner was my attempt to make legal AI actually useful rather than just impressive. But it also highlighted how much work is left to do, and it's directly connected to what I'm working on now in my research at Cornell.

My current work on enhancing reasoning capabilities in language models through reinforcement learning and test-time scaling builds on these same fundamental questions: How do we get AI systems to think systematically through problems instead of just finding the most likely answer?

And honestly? The access problem is what keeps me up at night. If we can build AI that makes legal research faster and cheaper, we might actually help close the justice gap. That's the real test of whether this work matters.
